Executive Summary

CookEase is a meal planning app designed for students who want to cook more but hit real, everyday barriers: time pressure, limited ingredients, low confidence, and decision fatigue.

This project was built from the ground up — starting with user research into Gen Z food behaviour and student cooking habits, competitive analysis across four products, and a heuristic audit surfacing 25 usability issues. All of that fed directly into five end-to-end user flows and a full hi-fi prototype.

The core insight: existing tools solve only one part of the journey. CookEase connects inspiration → planning → cooking in a single student-friendly experience.

The Challenge

Students consistently want to cook more — but don't. The research showed the barriers aren't about interest; they're about friction. Time, confidence, and decision effort are the real blockers.

Cooking confidence is the single strongest predictor of how often students cook. A well-designed tool has a real shot at changing behaviour — not just organising recipes. That was the brief.

Research findings
Key insights from Gen Z behaviour research
What the data told us about students and cooking
▾
  • Cooking confidence is the single strongest predictor of cooking frequency — more important than time or equipment
  • Roughly 30–40% of students experience food insecurity, making budget-aware design essential not optional
  • Gen Z frames food as experience and self-expression — tools need to feel motivating, not instructional
  • The biggest daily barrier is decision effort: "what should I cook tonight?" is genuinely exhausting without support
  • Students cook more when they can see visible outcomes — confidence built, money saved, meals made

Competitor Analysis

I analysed four products across direct and indirect competition — what each one does well, where it falls short, and what that means for CookEase.

Analysis
Four competitors, one market gap
Mealime, ReciMe, HelloFresh, DoorDash
▾
Mealime
Direct
Great automation — instant planning, auto grocery lists
Hands-free cooking mode with gesture control
No calendar or long-term planning
Creates dependency, doesn't build confidence
ReciMe
Direct
Best-in-class for importing social media recipes
Demo-led onboarding shows value immediately
No built-in recipes — assumes you know what you want
Doesn't solve "what should I eat?" at all
HelloFresh
Indirect
Removes planning friction entirely
Beautiful recipe cards, step-by-step instructions
Expensive and inflexible for students
Teaches reliance, not cooking skills
DoorDash
Indirect
Maximum convenience, familiar UX patterns
Expensive and unsustainable for students
Replaces cooking entirely — not a solution

The market gap: every existing tool solves one part of the journey. None connect inspiration → planning → cooking in a single beginner-friendly experience built for students.

Discovery — UX Audit

Full heuristic audit of the CookEase prototype using Nielsen's 10 heuristics, covering onboarding, meal planning, recipe browsing, shopping, and profile. 25 issues identified. Here are the most critical.

Critical
Calendar too small to use on mobile
High mis-tap risk, fails accessibility requirements
▾

The calendar grid was too dense for reliable touch interaction — users risked selecting the wrong date or meal on every tap. It also failed minimum touch target sizes.

Fix: Daily view showing "Your meals today" (breakfast, lunch, dinner) as the default, with a horizontal carousel for weekly — larger targets, simpler mental model.

Critical
Store selector with no clear purpose
Users couldn't understand what selecting a store actually did
▾

The shopping area had a store selector at the top with no explanation. Can you order here? See deals? The value was invisible.

Fix: Add clear helper text — "Select your store to see ingredient availability and current prices." Define the purpose before asking for input.

High
Browse by cuisine doesn't fit students
Students think in constraints, not culinary traditions
▾

Primary discovery categories were organised by cuisine type, which doesn't match how students choose what to cook. They ask "what's quick?" or "what's cheap?" — not "what's Italian?"

Fix: Replace primary categories with "Time Savers", "Budget Meals", "Trending", "Few Ingredients". Cuisine stays as a secondary filter.

High
Confusing "Add to Plan" flow
CTA disappeared off-screen, sequence was unclear
▾

The popup asked for date then time-of-day in a confusing sequence, and the "Select" button was pushed below the viewport — appearing broken to users.

Fix: CTA always visible in the modal, relabelled to "Save" for clarity. Calendar pattern consistent with the rest of the app.

High
Allergy selection overwhelming
Full allergen list is too long and creates drop-off
▾

Fix: Quick-select chips for the 8 most common allergies (nuts, dairy, gluten, shellfish, eggs, soy, sesame, fish) with a search field for everything else.

High
Disorganised profile structure
Settings arranged by system architecture, not user goals
▾

Fix: Reorganised into three groups: My Account (login, security), My History (lists, meal plans), My Preferences (dietary, shopping, stores).

User Flow Design

Five core flows designed to cover every main journey — each informed directly by the audit findings and the research.

Flow 1
Discover
Dual discovery — swipe and browse in one viewport
▾

Two discovery modes visible at the same time: Tinder-style swipe cards at the top for passive browsing, Netflix-style category rows below for intentional searching. The algorithm learns from swipes over time.

  • Swipe right → saves to Favourites automatically, no extra steps
  • Categories match student pain points: Time Savers, Budget Meals, Trending, Few Ingredients
  • Search with filters for dietary preferences, cooking time, skill level
Flow 2
Meal Plan
Daily-first view with weekly toggle — direct response to audit
▾
  • Default home shows "Today's meals" — breakfast, lunch, dinner cards
  • Weekly toggle uses a horizontal carousel, not a grid
  • Adding a recipe optionally prompts: "Add ingredients to grocery list?"
  • Plan up to 4 weeks ahead, with weekly meal prep notifications
Flow 3
Favourites
Auto-populated, zero extra steps
▾
  • Swipe right in Discover → saved automatically
  • Manual upload or URL import from any recipe website
  • "+" button adds to meal plan without navigating away
  • Renamed from "Recipes" to "Favourites" — aligns with industry standards
Flow 4
Groceries
Auto-generated from meal plan — biggest friction point removed
▾
  • Automatically compiled from the weekly meal plan — no manual transfer
  • Duplicates consolidated, quantities adjusted for servings
  • Organised by supermarket aisle for faster in-store shopping
  • Paste-entire-list feature for online supermarket checkout
Flow 5
Rewards
Habit loops and tangible motivation
▾
  • Points earned by cooking, rating, and uploading dish photos
  • Redeemable for kitchen utensils, gift cards, appliances in the Marketplace
  • Daily spin wheel adds a surprise element to keep engagement up
  • Cookie mascot adds personality — makes progress feel rewarding not clinical
  • Friends leaderboard drives friendly competition

Conclusion

CookEase started as a design brief and became a genuine product built on real evidence. Every decision — from the discovery categories to the calendar layout to the onboarding flow — came directly from research findings, competitive gaps, or specific audit issues.

The project showed how a rigorous research foundation doesn't slow down design — it speeds it up. When you know exactly what's broken and why, you stop guessing and start solving.
